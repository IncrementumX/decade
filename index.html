The assignment
Decade asks for an investment conviction on the role of Brazil’s tax-exempt assets — FIIs, Infra Debêntures, FI-Infra, FIAGRO, CRIs and CRAs (bank-issued LCI/LCA excluded) — in a client’s portfolio. The brief is explicit that these convictions both anchor client advice and teach the firm’s AI how it thinks about asset allocation and risk. Five questions must be answered, each supported by evidence with assumptions stated:
  1. Which types of tax-exempt assets should exist in the portfolio.
  2. Whether exposure should be implemented through direct instruments or funds (this may differ by asset type).
  3. How much should be allocated to each relevant bucket as a percentage of total wealth.
  4. Which specific assets are best-in-class to implement this exposure (actual assets).
  5. How client-specific factors (e.g. income stability, liquidity needs, existing real assets, risk tolerance) would impact your recommendations.
My Approach

Building a portfolio and underwriting across six different tax-exempt asset classes is not an easy task — especially in a country like Brazil. It requires a deep reading of the environment, depth where it actually matters, and relentless diligence. The content could easily become infinite.

Two mental models guided my work:

  • Reductionism: avoiding complexity loops — they generate entropy, energy that never converts into real work.
  • Reverse-thinking: starting each day thinking ex-ante about what went wrong — where to focus energy, which workflows broke — and ensuring it cannot happen in my process.

Statement 1 · The first mandate is survival
01

For any individual or family balance sheet, the first allocation mandate is survival. Whether the objective is absolute return, long-term purchasing power, or intergenerational wealth preservation, the portfolio must first avoid permanent impairment. This means diversifying risk, sizing adequation, and protecting the compounding base before trying to optimize returns.

"Rule No. 1: never lose money. Rule No. 2: never forget Rule No. 1."— Buffett's two rules
Statement 2 · Brazil makes fixed income the center of gravity
02

Pedro Malan often says that, in Brazil, even the past is uncertain — and he is right. We live with a deep inflationary memory — shaped by multiple stabilization plans and monetary regimes over time, structurally high real rates, recurring electoral binary risks, and shallow equity markets.

As a result, fixed income is the center of gravity of Brazilian allocation: the natural core of household portfolios, and almost the mirror image of the U.S., where households are primarily owners rather than creditors.

Statement 3 · Tax exemption changes the return equation
03

Tax-exempt assets are powerful portfolio-construction resources; they may promote diversification, high net risk-adjusted returns and inflation protection — if their pitfalls are avoided and they are assessed responsibly.

Their relevance starts with a simple point: Brazilian asset allocation must be evaluated on a net-of-tax basis. A taxable bond and a tax-exempt instrument may look similar on a gross yield basis, but they can produce materially different outcomes after income tax.

A simple example: a taxable CDB paying 100% of CDI, if held for 720 days, is equivalent to a tax-exempt instrument paying 82.5% of CDI; if held for more than 720 days, the breakeven rises to 85.0% of CDI. This is the core reason tax-exempt assets matter: the exemption can materially change the relative attractiveness of otherwise similar instruments.

An environment shaped by instability
Brazil is not a clean compounding environment. It is a high-carry, high-uncertainty economy marked by recurring fiscal stress, binary political events and inflationary memory. Before the Real Plan, the country burned through six currencies and a string of failed stabilization plans — the memory that still anchors high nominal rates as the central reference for local allocation.

So what
Brazil is not a passive compounding market. It is a market where investors need compensation for instability — and that compensation shows up first as a high local rate.
The price of uncertainty · rates and inflation
The clearest expression of that instability is the history of interest rates and inflation. The Selic is not just a policy variable — it is the opportunity cost every local asset must clear. High nominal and real rates are the market’s price for fiscal uncertainty and inflationary memory: the investor starts with a high local risk-free hurdle before taking any credit, duration or equity risk.
So what
Brazil’s high-rate regime is not an anomaly — it is the market’s standing compensation for structural uncertainty. That is the hurdle.
Returns per asset class · 2003–2026
This macro regime translated into an unusual return hierarchy. Historically, Brazil has rewarded carry and real-rate exposure more consistently than local equity beta. CDI compounded wealth at a high real rate with limited volatility, and Inflation-linked sovereign bonds went one step further: they converted Brazil’s structural uncertainty into long-term real return. Equities, by contrast, required much more patience and volatility tolerance, but did not dominate the compounding story, as it can be seen below.

So what
The relevant test for a tax-exempt asset is not “does it yield more?” It is “does it compensate enough for the extra risk versus CDI and NTN-Bs?”
Where are we right now?
The goal is not to forecast the next rate move — it is to read the present setup and calibrate risk against history. And the setup is unusual: Brazil is living through one of the highest real-rate regimes of the last two decades. The NTN-B real curve sits around 7.5–8.0% across maturities — the 5Y real yield near the 86th percentile of its own 20-year history and the 10Y near the 87th. The investor is being paid a structurally rich real coupon to hold sovereign duration: locking roughly 8% real has been available only in the most stressed windows since 2004.
This cuts two ways for allocation. It makes inflation-linked carry — NTN-Bs and exempt IPCA+ credit — especially attractive, because the real entry point is near a 20-year high and the tax exemption compounds on top of an already-rich real yield. But it also raises the bar for everything else: when the risk-free real rate is this high, lower-quality credit and equity beta must clear an unusually demanding hurdle before they earn a place — high real rates are an opportunity in sovereign real assets and a filter for everything riskier.
Conditional forward real returns · by entry NTN-B real yield
How to read: the “entry yield” is the real yield-to-maturity on a 5-year NTN-B (Tesouro IPCA+) — the inflation-linked federal bond — at the moment of purchase; the bar is the average annualized real return earned over the next 5 years (measured on the IMA-B5+ index of long NTN-Bs), and the whisker is its best-to-worst range. Splitting entries into six yield buckets — the granularity needed to see where today’s ~8% sits — the relationship is monotone: the more you are paid in real yield at entry, the higher and more reliably positive your next five years.
So what
The current setup favors real-rate carry — but selectivity matters: high sovereign real yields raise the bar for credit beta.
What the landscape tells us
Although this case is about building a framework for tax-exempt assets, the landscape makes one thing unavoidable: for an onshore (BRL) portfolio you cannot ignore the compounding power of CDI and IMA-B. Across 2003–2026 these taxable government benchmarks out-compounded local equity beta in nominal, real and risk-adjusted terms, and they set the hurdle every exempt asset must clear after tax. The tax-exempt sleeve is built on top of this base — not instead of it.
CDI and real-rate govies are the gravitational center of the onshore portfolio; tax exemption is how we try to beat their after-tax hurdle — not a reason to abandon them.
From gross returns to net returns · the tax wedge
Once the local hurdle is defined, tax becomes central. In a high-rate economy the gap between gross and net is not a detail: a taxable bond and a tax-exempt asset are not comparable on headline yield — only on after-tax real return, adjusted for credit, liquidity, duration, governance and structure risk. Exemption lowers the return hurdle required to compound real wealth; it is a tool, not a thesis.

01

Overview

Definition & tax
Definition
FIIs are Brazilian closed-end real-estate investment funds (broadly comparable to U.S. REITs), typically traded on the Brazilian stock exchange through quotas, that provide investors with indirect exposure to the real-estate market.
Underlying exposure

Varies materially across vehicles, as will be seen below:

  • Equity FIIs own income-generating real assets, such as malls, logistics warehouses and office buildings.
  • Credit / paper FIIs invest primarily in real-estate credit instruments, especially CRIs.
  • Hybrid portfolios combine both strategies.
  • FOFs (funds-of-funds) — FIIs can also be accessed through them.
Structuring
  • Closed-end vehicles — quotaholders do not have a standing redemption right against the fund.
  • Liquidity, when available, is typically obtained through exit in the secondary market.
Tax regimeTax efficiency is concentrated in income distributions, not in total return.
Income distributions Exempt

For Brazilian individuals, distributed income may be tax-exempt, provided:

  • the fund is traded on an exchange or organized OTC market;
  • it has at least 50 quotaholders;
  • the investor does not hold 10% or more of the quotas or economic rights to the fund's income.
Capital gains 20%
  • Gains realized upon the sale of FII quotas are taxable, currently at a 20% rate on net gains.
  • As a result, FIIs are primarily tax-efficient income vehicles, while quota prices remain fully exposed to market risk.
Distribution policy
  • By law, FIIs must distribute at least 95% of profits calculated on a cash basis, based on semi-annual financial statements dated June 30 and December 31.
  • In practice, listed FIIs typically distribute income monthly, although the statutory distribution requirement is semi-annual — relevant for investors seeking recurring cash income and a more explicit pass-through of real-estate cash flows.
Benchmark
  • Equity FIIs — IFIX (B3's real-estate fund index, designed to track the average performance of listed real-estate funds).
  • Credit FIIs — managers adopt CDI or IPCA + spreads.
02

Value chain

How it is built

How a FII is originated, structured and monetized — from the underlying real asset or receivable through to the cash that reaches the quotaholder.

1

Underlying asset / receivable

The economic exposure sits in either real assets or real-estate credit instruments.

2

Origination / structuring

The opportunity is originated by an asset owner, developer or borrower, and structured by the manager and related service providers into an investable fund exposure.

3

FII vehicle

The FII is the closed-end listed vehicle that holds the assets and concentrates the underlying cash flows.

4

B3-listed quotas

Investors access the fund through exchange-traded quotas, typically buying and selling them in the secondary market.

5

Investor distributions

Underlying cash flows — rent, interest, monetary correction, amortization or realized gains — are collected by the fund, netted for expenses, and distributed to quotaholders.

Guardrails · ranges + caps, not final weights
Concentration caps
Per-issuer cap (single CRI / CRA / debênture) and per-class cap. FII ≤ ~40% of the exempt sleeve — beyond that, drawdown and risk-adjusted return both deteriorate (the Aggressive read). FIAGRO held to a satellite ≤8% of the sleeve.
Illiquidity budget
Cap gated / illiquid sleeves (open-end FI-Infra with redemption queues, pulverized CRA, FIDC-taxed structures) so the occasional-access need is always met from the CDI buffer + listed / secondary-liquid names. Conservative keeps a ~24%-of-sleeve cash buffer; do not let it fall below the access need.
Duration budget
The carry core (IMA-B5+ proxy) is long-duration — it drives both the return and the drawdown. Size the long-duration sleeve to a duration ceiling so a real-rate shock (the −20.9% IMA-B5+ drawdown) stays inside tolerance. A CDI+ credit satellite is a duration-light offset.
Exit-value caveat
NAV-smoothed / illiquid credit understates true mark-to-market vol. The proxy indices (IFIX, IMA-B family) are listed / liquid and show more honest vol than a closed FI-Infra or direct-CRI book would print. Real exit value under stress (gate, bid-ask, queue) can be worse than the proxy DD suggests — do NOT read the proxy vol / DD as the floor for an illiquid implementation.
Rules-engine precedence
Sizing ranges feed the rules engine; its precedence (eligibility → portfolio-fit → concentration / liquidity caps) governs — NOT a point optimizer. Mean-variance over NAV-smoothed credit self-invalidates (vol illusion).
Bottom line (decision-grade)
Within the onshore exempt sleeve, on observed proxies, 2004–2026 was a regime where CDI was a punishingly high hurdle — a diversified exempt-FI book beat cash only modestly in real terms while taking real drawdown. The case is risk-adjusted, not return-maximizing: a Conservative mix delivers ~IMA-B5+ real return at materially lower vol and drawdown. FII adds diversification but is the drawdown driver. The decisive caveats are illiquidity / exit-value and the per-name data gap: the proxy backtest cannot price manager alpha or the exempt tax edge, and an illiquid implementation can exit worse than the listed proxies print. No final sizing is set here; ranges and guardrails only, pending per-name total-return data and CRI/CRA spread validation. Percentage-of-wealth sizing and the model portfolio are completed in the next round.
