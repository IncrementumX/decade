What this tool measures

For a holding period H (1, 3, 5 or 10 years) the tool slides an H-year window one month at a time from your start date to the most recent complete window. In each window it compounds the monthly returns of asset A and asset B, then asks a single question: did A end ahead of B? The win-rate is the share of windows where the answer is yes. The excess is A’s annualized compound return minus B’s, in percentage points per year.

Nominal, real, or USD

The toggle re-expresses every monthly return before the windows are built:

  • Nominal BRL — the series as reported.
  • Real (IPCA) — each monthly return is deflated by that month’s IPCA, so the comparison is in purchasing-power terms. IPCA itself is available as an asset, so “beat IPCA” means delivered a positive real return.
  • USD (PTAX) — BRL total return is converted at the end-of-month PTAX rate, so the gap absorbs currency moves. USD/BRL is available as an asset to isolate how much of the local return the currency took back.

Why the caveat matters

Rolling windows one month apart share 119 of their 120 months, so a string of “wins” can come from one lucky decade counted many times. Over 10-year horizons there are only about twelve genuinely independent windows in this sample. The win-rates are an honest description of 2004–2026; they are not a probability statement about the next decade. They are kept visible on every view for that reason.

How to read the equity result

The default matchup — IBOV vs CDI over 10 years — is the case in one number: Brazilian equity beat cash in only 16% of rolling decades, because a CDI compounding near double digits is a punishing hurdle. Switch B to IPCA and the equity picture improves; switch A to IMA-B and the inflation-linked bond clears CDI in essentially every 10-year window. That asymmetry is the backbone of the tax-exempt case.
