From gross returns to net returns · the tax wedge
Once the local hurdle is defined, tax becomes central. In a high-rate economy the gap between gross and net is not a detail: a taxable bond and a tax-exempt asset are not comparable on headline yield — only on after-tax real return, adjusted for credit, liquidity, duration, governance and structure risk. Exemption lowers the return hurdle required to compound real wealth; it is a tool, not a thesis.

CDI / taxable fixed income

Base
Accrued income / gains
Rate
Regressive table 22.5% → 15% by holding period
Timing
At redemption / sale
Holding
Longer hold → lower rate
Caveat
IOF applies on redemptions within 30 days.

NTN-B / Tesouro Direto

Base
Coupons, sale & maturity gains
Rate
Same regressive 22.5% → 15% schedule
Timing
On coupon, sale or maturity
Holding
Bracket set by holding period
Caveat
Held direct to maturity: no come-cotas — a compounding edge.

IBOV TR / equities

Base
Capital gains; dividends & JCP separate
Rate
~15% cash-market gains (day-trade differs)
Timing
On sale (monthly for cash market)
Holding
Monthly exemption may apply to small sales
Caveat
IBOV TR is a benchmark, not the investor’s after-tax return.

Funds / come-cotas

Base
Fund NAV gains
Rate
15% (long) / 20% (short), semiannual advance
Timing
Come-cotas every May & Nov
Holding
Erodes compounding before redemption
Caveat
Applies to many open-ended FI / multimarket funds.

FIIs & exempt income

Base
Distributions (if legal tests met)
Rate
0% on qualifying distributions for individuals
Timing
On distribution
Holding
Exemption is on income, not price
Caveat
Capital gains on FII sales are still taxed (~20%).

So what
The same gross return can translate into very different net outcomes depending on asset type, tax rule and holding period.
