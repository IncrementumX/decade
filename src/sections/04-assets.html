What it is & how the exemption works

One breath. A FII is an exchange-listed, daily-liquid closed-end fund (Lei 8.668/1993; CVM Resolution 175) that holds either physical real estate (tijolo) or a pool of real-estate-backed credit (CRIs — papel), and distributes monthly income that is PF income-tax exempt under conditions (≥95% of semiannual accounting profit must be distributed). The investor owns a cota: a fractional, exchange-traded claim on the fund’s net assets — never direct title to a building or to any single CRI.

Why FIIs are not fixed income. The exemption is a wrapper on the income leg only; the underlying is either listed real-estate equity (cap rate, vacancy, asset value) or outsourced CRI underwriting. Quotas are exchange-marked, so they carry real price volatility and drawdowns (IFIX max drawdown −22% since 2011). Underwriting a FII starts by deciding which return engine you are buying.

The two-leg tax split (the central insight). Income / distributions are EXEMPT (0%) for qualifying individuals (Lei 11.033/2004 art. 3, II, as amended by Lei 14.754/2023); capital gain on a cota sale is TAXED 20% flat, monthly DARF, with no R$20k/month relief (that applies to stocks, not FII cotas). The exemption conditions: listed-only trading; ≥100 quotaholders (raised from 50 by Lei 14.754/2023, effective 01-Jan-2024 — older materials still print 50; the law prevails); the individual holds <10% of quotas and income; the related group holds <30%. For a R$5mm resident PF in large, liquid FIIs these are typically non-binding — but verify holder count in smaller funds.

The mismatch that drives selection. Because exemption covers income but not gain, the wrapper is most tax-efficient when the thesis is recurring income, least efficient when the thesis is NAV re-rating. A rational PF is structurally biased toward income-payers (high-grade paper, logistics, mature malls at fair P/VP) and against appreciation/turnaround plays: a deep-discount office FII closing a 0.72x→1.0x gap hands ~20% of the upside to Receita. The wrapper itself selects the strategy.

Value chain & where the conflicts sit

The chain: sponsor / asset manager → fund (cota) → B3 listing → ~95% retail holders. Two structural conflicts decide governance risk. First, the fee-on-AUM, emission-driven incentive: a manager paid on assets is rewarded for growing the fund through follow-on emissions, which can dilute incumbents if priced below NAV or used to buy non-accretive assets — the single biggest minority-value-transfer risk in tijolo. Second, related-party origination (a sponsor selling its own developments into the fund). Both break the alignment between the manager and the cotista. The defenses are concrete: rated, low-fee structures (HGBS: brAA+, 0.60% management fee, no performance fee), an across-cycle track record, and emission history that has been accretive rather than dilutive.

The market is consolidating toward REITs — and a manager has operationally started it. Patria frames Brazil entering a “ciclo de consolidação”: the US went from >800 REITs since 1960 to fewer than 200 today, and of 400+ listed FIIs “many will no longer be relevant and will be absorbed” — with the counter-intuitive corollary that consolidation increases real diversification (one fund holding dozens of assets beats a retail investor holding dozens of thin monoativos). Hedge took the operational first step: getting HGBS11 admitted to the FTSE EPRA Nareit Global REIT Index (the first Brazilian FII), which required English financials and the 75%-EBITDA-from-equity test. The implication for selection: buy scale, liquidity and governance — those are the consolidation survivors and the FTSE-eligible names — and front-run the passive foreign REIT inflows.

Return engine & benchmarks

Total return = monthly distribution yield (exempt for PF) + price return on the cota (real equity / duration risk, gain taxed 20%). The income leg is the tax-advantaged compounding engine; the price leg carries the risk and the tax. Over 2015–2025 IFIX roughly matched CDI but with real drawdowns where CDI never falls — FIIs are not a CDI substitute. The dispersion is the rate call: IFIX out-earns CDI in cutting/benign years (2016, 2019, 2025) and loses in hiking/stress years (2020, 2021, 2024 all negative).

Where you enter matters. Within the IFIX era (2011–2025), entering when the NTN-B real yield was in its top tercile (>6.04%) was historically followed by the strongest IFIX returns — a 12m forward mean of 15.2% (vs 2.4% in the low tercile ≤5.16%) and a 36m mean of 13.8% (vs 3.4%). High real rates compress cap-rate and paper-duration entry valuations, and the subsequent re-rating is the return. The current 8.18% (Jun-2026) is above the entire IFIX-era range (max 7.97%, Feb-2025), so it has no completed forward window yet — descriptive, not predictive, and on overlapping monthly windows. The 6-bucket real-rate regime view (<4 / 4–5 / 5–6 / 6–7 / 7–8 / >8) used elsewhere in this case lives in the Landscape regime chart; the IFIX-clean window simply never reached the extreme buckets, which is why the entry-regime read here is stated as terciles rather than re-cut into empty 6-bucket bars.

IPCA paper (papel) — selective, size for duration. A diversified pool of inflation-linked (IPCA+) high-grade CRIs — a longer-duration CRI credit fund. Hybrids — avoid; FoFs — avoid. Hybrids trade transparency for convenience: even a single large “credit” FII can be 64% a FoF — CPTS11 is 63.9% FIIs (78 funds) + 24.8% CRIs, a discount-arbitrage overlay whose risk means underwriting 78 funds you cannot see cleanly. FoFs add a second fee layer on top of the underlying funds’ fees plus an overlap-control problem, for exposure the persona can assemble directly. Neither is a structural core bucket; use only on a very specific, well-governed manager case (VGHF11 / MXRF11 named as reference only, not endorsed).

Structuring — direct vs fund
When direct works

Effectively never for FIIs — and that is the point. The only “direct” choice is which listed ticker. Buying a building directly forfeits daily liquidity and the FII income-tax wrapper (direct rental income is taxed) and demands hands-on asset/tenant management. Buying individual CRIs directly forfeits diversification (~20–40 names needed), per-name covenant / garantia / servicer monitoring, and liquidity (OTC, R$1mn+ lots, often hold-to-maturity) — a different product, analyzed in the CRI section.

When a fund is the route

Almost always: the listed FII ticker IS the fund implementation. One ticket buys instant diversification (a papel FII = ~90 CRIs; a tijolo FII = a multi-asset, multi-tenant portfolio), professional anchored underwriting, daily liquidity at ~R$100/cota (a R$5mm sleeve across 5–8 liquid names builds / unwinds in days), and monthly exempt income with MtM transparency. Fees of 0.60–1.60% are a real drag but far cheaper and less risky than solo origination; net exempt DY is still ~12–16% on papel. The structuring decision collapses to ticker selection within each subtype.

Investment conviction & client fit

The verdict — Selective. FIIs belong in the onshore exempt sleeve selectively, underwritten subtype-by-subtype and name-by-name, never as one generic bucket and never IFIX-bought-blindly. The split is mandatory:

  1. Equity / tijolo earns its place as exempt real-estate income with real-asset beta (logistics + quality malls), underwritten asset-by-asset.
  2. Credit / papel earns its place as diversified, liquid, exempt access to CRI credit (CDI core-capable + IPCA selective) — benchmarked as credit, and netted against any direct-CRI bucket as one consolidated CRI-credit exposure (do not size papel FIIs as “real estate” and direct CRI as “credit” and sum them).
  3. Offices are avoid as core (premium-only turnaround — taxed gain, uncovered DY); hybrids are avoid (transparency traded for convenience); FoFs are avoid (double fee).
  4. FIIs are not substitutes for CDI or “safe fixed income.” They are exchange-marked, draw down ~−22%, their Sharpe vs CDI ≈ 0, and their gain is taxed. The exempt income is the reason to own them; the price leg is the risk to underwrite.

Client fit — fits if the client wants exempt monthly income with daily liquidity and real-asset / real-estate-credit diversification; accepts genuine price volatility and ~−22% drawdown in exchange for honest marks and the current ~0.89x NAV-discount optionality; has a recurring-income thesis (not NAV re-rating); and does not already concentrate in direct real estate. Reduce if the rate cycle turns clearly hawkish; discounts compress to/above NAV without fundamental improvement; the credit-FII allocation would double-count an existing direct-CRI sleeve; or fees / emissions erode the net exempt carry. Avoid if the thesis relies on the taxed capital-gain leg; the instrument is an opaque hybrid / FoF with no specific manager case; the name is in the thin tail; or FIIs are being used as a bond / cash substitute (category error).

What it is & how the exemption works

One breath. A debênture issued by an infrastructure SPE / concessionária / permissionária / autorizatária / arrendatária under Lei 12.431 to fund a priority infrastructure project declared by federal decree. Economically it is senior (usually) project / corporate credit on a regulated or contracted cash flow — transmission RAP (receita anual permitida, availability-based, near-bond-like), generation PPAs, toll-road tariffs, sanitation tariffs, telecom / fiber, ports — almost always IPCA + a fixed coupon, 5–15y, often amortizing. The investor owns either (a) direct — a single debênture (single-name credit claim), or (b) indirect — a cota of an FI-Infra fund holding 60–140 such bonds.

The exemption (the prize). Under Lei 12.431/2011 Art. 2, I, the resident PF income-tax rate is literally 0% (“alíquota … 0% … quando auferidos por pessoa física”), and capital gain on a secondary sale is ALSO exempt (0%) — the uniquely broad both-legs-zero treatment, broader than FII (gain 20%) or CRI/CRA (gain 15%). The benefit attaches to the instrument, not the holder’s status.

Do not confuse the two laws — the single most common error in this space. Lei 12.431/2011 → PF exempt (income + gain), in scope. Regulatory risk, surfaced not buried. MP 1.303/2025 proposed a 5% tax on this exempt income; it lapsed / was rejected by the Câmara (Oct-2025), so the exemption is maintained for 2026 and preserved by Lei 15.270/2025. During the threat, Idex-Infra spreads moved ~35 bps and demand reversed — the wrapper’s value is reflexive and politically contingent, and the threat recurs.

Value chain & where the subsidy goes

The chain: infrastructure SPE (issuer) → arranger / intermediário → FI-Infra funds and intermediaries (the dominant buyers) → PF holder. Two structural facts decide who captures the subsidy and who sets the price. First, most of the subsidy is issuer-captured, not investor-captured: a 12.431 issuer raises cheaper funding because the exemption lets the buyer accept a lower gross yield — for AAA names the spread over NTN-B is driven negative, so the investor pays the wrapper’s value forward to the issuer. Second, the buyer base is now fund / intermediary-dominated: ANBIMA documents that “Fundos de Investimento” rose to ~27% of primary subscription (from ~7% in 2018) and intermediaries 37–57%, while direct PF in the primary stays below 2%. Most PF exposure is intermediated through FI-Infra funds, so the bond’s price is set by fund flows, not by buy-and-hold individuals — when flows reverse, the marginal price-setter disappears. Sparta tracked ~R$14bn of infra-fund redemptions in May-2026 and, more worryingly, a collapse in gross inflows.

  • The most complete wrapper in the set — income and capital gain exempt (vs FII 20% / CRI-CRA 15% on the gain).

  • For AAA the gross spread over NTN-B is ~0 / negative — the investor pays the wrapper’s value forward to the issuer; this is not a credit-alpha trade.

  • The entire edge rests on a politically contingent exemption (MP 1.303 5% threat moved spreads ~35 bps; it recurs).

Structuring — direct vs fund
When direct works

Only at scale, with genuine credit capacity, and on the safest names where the fund adds least. Justified when the investor has (a) size to hold 20–40 names for diversification, (b) underwriting capacity to read concession / project risk, (c) appetite for thin secondary liquidity, and (d) targets clean AAA transmission / toll / distribution names — on AAA names yielding NTN-B +0/−16 bps, the ~1% fund fee is the main thing being recaptured. A self-originated direct book of sanitation / single-asset credits is inappropriate at R$5mm: adverse selection (a PF buying secondary sees the deals the desks passed on), no diversification, monitoring burden and thin exit.

When a fund is the route

Almost always at R$5mm. One listed ticket buys 60–140 names, underwriting outsourced to a credit desk, with daily B3 liquidity and both legs exempt — the fund earns its fee on credit selection across a cycle. Listed-closed structurally beats open-end this cycle: open-end funds in a redemption wave are forced sellers into widening spreads (D+0 apply / D+21 redeem on the open-end Itaú vehicle), while listed-closed cotas trade intraday and can buy the dislocation — carrying instead a NAV-discount basis risk. The new buyback (“recompra de cotas”) regulation wave signals that persistent NAV discounts are an expected feature of the listed structure. Prefer the real-rate (IPCA+) listed funds over the CDI-hedged sleeve.

Investment conviction & client fit

The verdict — Invest, cautiously. Infra debêntures earn a core role — implemented through listed, closed, real (IPCA+) FI-Infra funds, not a self-originated direct book. Four load-bearing points:

01Overview & tax

What it is. A FI-Infra is a fund (CVM Resolution 175, classe Incentivada em Infraestrutura) that holds ≥85% of net assets in incentivized infra debêntures issued under Lei 12.431/2011. The underlying economic risk is identical to direct 12.431 debêntures — concession credit across transmission, generation, toll roads, sanitation, telecom. The wrapper adds diversification (one ticket buys 60–140 names), an outsourced credit desk, secondary liquidity through listed cotas, and an optional CDI hedge overlay — for a 0.75–1.10% fee. The investor owns cotas, never the debêntures directly. FI-Infra is therefore not a new asset class; it is a tax-exempt vehicle around an existing one, and the real choices are implementation: (a) duration class — IPCA+ real-rate versus CDI-hedged; (b) structure — listed-closed versus open-end; (c) credit-quality composition; (d) fee drag against the captured spread.

Tax — and why the exemption is durable. Income distributed to the PF cotista is exempt (0% IR), by pass-through of the underlying-debenture exemption under Lei 12.431/2011 art. 3. The exemption is a property of the 2011 statute attached to the debenture, not a fund-level perk a fund-tax reform could remove. The cleanest evidence of how hard that carve-out is to touch: the newer Lei 14.801/2024 ("debêntures de infraestrutura") deliberately created a parallel instrument that does not carry the PF exemption — its benefits sit with the issuer (IRPJ/CSLL deduction) and the foreign investor (withholding cut to 15%). The regulator chose to broaden infra funding without extending the costly PF subsidy, confirming the 12.431 exemption is treated as a discrete carve-out rather than a loose loophole. The exemption survived the MP 1.303/2025 tax threat (which lapsed unvoted) and is explicitly preserved under Lei 15.270/2025. It remains a policy variable, not an asset property — but a well-anchored one. One caveat: the income exemption is unambiguous; the treatment of a cota capital gain on sale is fund-structure-specific and must be confirmed per regulamento before any total-return-with-appreciation model.

04Return engine

Takeaway. Return = IPCA accrual (or CDI for hedged funds) + carry/credit spread over NTN-B + real-rate mark-to-market (REAL only) − fee − hedge cost (CDI). The single most important framing: the "extra yield" versus a plain NTN-B is mostly the tax exemption, not credit. The market-wide IDA-Infra spread makes the point unmistakably — through 2025 it was negative, reaching a historic low of about −50bps in Oct-2025, because demand-led flow (+R$49bn into the funds in 2025) bid AAA infra paper through par, so it yielded less than an equivalent NTN-B. The MP-1.303 tax threat reversed the flow and the spread swung positive in 2026, to about +52bps mid-May then settling near +48bps. Even now an AAA-only basket prices roughly 26bps — thinner than most fund fees. The negative-spread era is the proof that "high carry" was a flow artifact, not credit compensation; the credit premium is real only where credit risk (sanitation, distressed concessions) is real.

The hedged "carry" trap, quantified. Every FI-Infra, hedged or not, is quoted with a carry in IPCA+ terms for comparability. But a CDI-hedged fund swaps that IPCA+ stream into CDI, so the delivered carry is the post-swap residual — which CDII11's own May-2026 report puts at only CDI+0.7% a.a., compressed from a CDI+2.0% target. Never present a hedged fund's IPCA+ carry as its yield; quote hedged funds in CDI+ and real-rate funds in IPCA+/NTN-B+spread, and never mix them.

Gross-up bridge. An exempt yield grosses up by 1/(1−t). At a 12% exempt distribution and t=15% the taxable-equivalent is 14.12%; at t=17.5% (the post-reform RF rate) it is 14.55%; at t=20%, 15.0%. The reform RF rate moving toward ~17.5% flat raises the exemption's relative value — a tailwind to the whole exempt-credit complex. The named cycle gauge to watch is the ANBIMA IDA-Infra spread over NTN-B: the cleanest free index of where the class sits in its credit cycle.

Yet on a 12-month basis in that same report it returned +6.04% versus CDI +11.45%. The reconciliation: a real-rate FI-Infra is structurally a levered long-real-duration position with a tax-free coupon attached — it should be sized as a view on the real-yield curve, not as a stable income sleeve.

06Key variables & risks

Spread is a risk thermometer, not a yield to maximize. The cross-section flatly inverts the naive "buy the highest carry" instinct. The two widest-spread listed funds are the two worst performers: XPID11 (spread ~1,038bps, since-inception +1.2%, only 6 assets, qualified-investor-only) and RBIF11 (560bps, since-inception −27%, R$76mn and illiquid). Against them, the highest-quality general-investor funds carry the tightest spreads — IFRA11 ~53bps, CDII11 trading through the NTN-B on a hedged basis, KDIF11 ~162bps. As Sparta puts it, "o spread é um termômetro de risco… carteiras mais defensivas e de maior qualidade tendem a carregar spreads menores." A spread far above the ~150–350bps cluster is a distress flag, not a value signal; the P/VPA discount on those names is the market correctly pricing impairment.

07Structuring: direct vs fund

Verdict. For a R$5mm onshore PF, FI-Infra funds dominate direct incentivized debêntures for the core infra-credit sleeve. A self-built direct book needs 20–40 names to diversify single-name and sanitation risk — impractical to assemble and monitor at this size given small per-issue lots. A direct AAA basket is a satellite only at scale and with genuine credit-DD capacity, and even then the value is diversification, liquidity and exemption-admin, not spread capture: for AAA paper the fee can exceed the captured ~26bps spread.

When direct works
  • At scale and with credit-DD capacity, to recapture the 0.85–1.10% fee on the safest AAA concession names where the fund adds least.
  • When the investor wants a specific, clean concession credit held to maturity and can size single-name concentration acceptably.
  • Never for a concentrated book of fewer than ~10 single-name debêntures (especially sanitation) — under-diversified, illiquid name-by-name, monitoring-heavy for a thin AAA spread.
When a fund is the route
  • The default for R$5mm: diversification (one ticket = 60–140 names), outsourced name-specific credit DD, secondary liquidity, and an optional CDI hedge overlay.
  • Prefer listed-closed over open-end — the listed structure is redemption-protected and lets a patient buyer capture the open-end's dislocation as a discount rather than suffer it as forced selling.
  • For spread/credit selection (sanitation, reach-for-spread) the desk's underwriting justifies the fee; for AAA-only the fee buys access and liquidity, not spread.

  • Reach-for-spread / distressed (XPID11, RBIF11) — avoid. Wide spread is impaired credit priced for losses, not alpha.

01Overview & tax

What it is. A CRI is a real-estate receivable wrapped in a securitization: a Termo de Securitização creates a patrimônio separado with a regime fiduciário, a garantia stack (alienação fiduciária of the property, cessão fiduciária of receivables, fundo de reserva, aval, coverage covenants), an agente fiduciário, and sometimes a servicer. It is not a bond — it is a contract bundle. For a resident individual the coupon income is exempt (Lei 11.033/2004); a capital gain on the certificate is taxed 15% (with the R$20k/month PF capital-gains relief that CRI/CRA enjoy). The role, if any, is exempt income held to maturity, not trading.

The mental model most PFs hold is wrong. “CRI = a diversified pool of many little home loans” does not describe the modern market. Recent CRI is overwhelmingly concentrado lastro — single- or few-obligor corporate real-estate credit (sale-leaseback, developer debentures, built-to-suit rent) — so a given CRI’s risk tracks one company, not a housing-default statistic. The high-yield tail is something else again: home-equity pools and half-built residential development, marked down in real time inside the funds that hold it.

High-grade Credit FIIs (fundos de papel) deliver the same exempt real-estate-credit income with diversification that absorbs single-name defaults, a desk that screens on garantias and management rather than headline yield, daily cota liquidity, and internalized reinvestment — for ~0.8–1.0% all-in against an IPCA+9–12% gross carry. The choice is not “fund or bonds” but which fund: favor high-grade, low-leverage managers over the high-yield/compromissada tail.

Net. Direct CRI clears only a very high bar and is not needed: diversified exempt exposure already arrives through Credit FIIs. A direct-CRI sleeve and a paper-FII sleeve are the same economic exposure and must be consolidated as one CRI-credit line, never summed as “credit” plus “real estate.”

Portfolio Construction. CRI exposure is sized inside the Credit-FII (papel) sleeve, and any direct-CRI exception is adjudicated against that same line. The class is underwritten here; it is picked and sized there.

01Overview & tax

What it is. A CRA is a fixed-income security issued by a securitizadora, representing a payment claim backed by agribusiness credit rights. It is not "renda fixa isenta" generica — it is a securitization wrapper, and the economics are whatever sits in the lastro: a single brAAA corporate debenture, a pool of producer receivables, a trading company's working-capital paper. Created by Lei 11.076/2004, governed by Lei 14.430/2022 and tightened by CMN Resolução 5.118 (Feb-2024). The single most important fact for an allocator is that CRA credit risk is bimodal: by volume the market is large investment-grade single-corporate paper; by stress the damage is concentrated in the pulverized-producer and agro-input-distributor tail. A block-NO on "all CRAs" is a category error; a block-YES on "tax-free agro" is the behavioral trap.

The edge is selection, not the exemption. The best deep-research framing is the one the section adopts: the advantage comes from three layers — directed funding to a politically-favored sector, the PF income exemption that lifts the net rate versus a taxable debenture, and a complexity/illiquidity premium — but the common error is to compare net rates only. The correct analysis is net spread versus an equivalent NTN-B / taxable debenture, quality of the lastro, subordination and guarantees, covenants, liquidity, and recovery capacity. The edge in CRA is to select structure and avoid the distributed bomb sold as tax-free fixed income.

Tax — income only. PF income / distributions are exempt (0% IR) under Lei 11.033/2004 art. 3 IV. The capital gain on a secondary sale is taxed at 15% — treat 15% as the planning rate. Unlike infra-debêntures (Lei 12.431, uniquely both legs exempt for PF), the CRA gain is taxed, which structurally biases the wrapper toward hold-for-carry over trade-the-paper. Via a FIDC / FIAGRO-FIDC route, income is taxed — that route fails the exempt thesis on tax grounds alone. The exemption is a legislated subsidy (Lei 11.033/2004), preserved under Lei 15.270/2025 and after the MP-1.303/2025 tax attempt lapsed (Oct-2025), but it remains a political/fiscal variable at the class level.

The regulatory tightening is structurally good for a selective buyer. CMN 5.118/2024 (reinforced by 5.163 and 5.212/2025) bars CRI/CRA whose devedor/codevedor/garantidor sits outside the applicable sector — the regulator closing the tax-exemption arbitrage that let non-agro corporates raise via engineered lastro. Two-sided read: it removed the worst creative-lastro adverse-selection deals from supply, raising the average quality of new IG-corporate issuance (a tailwind for a selective buyer); and it is part of why CRA issuance is range-bound rather than booming.

02Value chain

Agribusiness receivables are wrapped by a securitizadora into a CRA, distributed via public or OTC offer, and bought by the PF who owns the certificate directly with single-name credit risk. The securitizadora is only a vehicle — the relevant risk is the patrimônio separado / lastro / devedor, not the issuer name. EcoAgro dominates CRA issuance (108 offers, R$42.9bn in the 2024+ window; Virgo R$22.3bn; Opea R$15.6bn), but issuer concentration tells you nothing about underlying credit.

  • Single-obligor concentration — many CRAs are one large agro corporate; granular credit DD is essential, and the securitizadora's name is not the credit.

03Market structure

Takeaway. The CRA market is large, growing and increasingly retail — and none of those facts is an attractiveness verdict. Issuance hit a record R$46.2bn in 2025 (+11.1% YoY) on the ANBIMA cut; estoque reached R$119bn integralized / R$182bn lastros across 682 CRAs (Oct-2025); and retail now holds a record 25% of the estoque. The recovered CVM registered-offer series (a proxy for primary public offers, not estoque) shows CRA scaling from R$10bn (2019) to a R$52.8bn peak (2023), a 2024 pullback to R$43bn, and a 2025 recovery to R$47.9bn — a market more sensitive to the agro credit cycle and the lastro rules than CRI. Size does not equal suitability; the caution is earned by underwriting complexity, not the distribution share.

The deal-size math is the structural surprise. CRA = R$50.2bn across 184 deals = R$272.8mn average, versus CRI's R$85.6mn average — about 3.2x the CRI deal and the largest of the three securitized classes. The CRA market is not "thousands of tiny farmer receivables": value is concentrated in a small number of large IG-corporate obligors (JBS/Seara, São Martinho, traders, agroindustrials). The granular-producer tail is a minority of value but the majority of headline risk — which is exactly what supports a "selective direct = IG-corporate" stance.

04Return engine

Takeaway. A CRA is an IPCA+ (or CDI-linked) carry instrument: the investor earns a maturity-matched real-rate floor (NTN-B) + a credit spread, paid as exempt income to PF, principal amortized over the life, less any default loss. The exemption raises the net yield versus an equivalent taxable debenture — but part of that subsidy is captured by the issuer as cheaper funding, so the observed gross spread is thin. The role is carry, not appreciation; the gain on early sale is a separate taxed event.

The load-bearing subsidy insight. An IG agro corporate raises exempt CRA below its taxable-debenture cost, so the observed CRA spread already embeds that transfer. Consequence: a thin gross spread can still beat a taxable peer after gross-up yet be unattractive after illiquidity. Do not read a tight IG CRA spread as "low compensation, avoid" — gross it up first; but do not read a wide pulverized spread as "alpha" — it is priced for losses. Reference yields, Jun-2026: NTN-B 2032 ~IPCA+8.30% sets the floor; IG CRAs print ~IPCA+7.6–7.8% (Seara, Marfrig) — trading through the long NTN-B on a gross basis, with the exemption carrying the after-tax case.

The IG-corporate CRA is best valued as a tax-exempt corporate bond. Compute the net spread versus an equivalent NTN-B or taxable debenture, then ask whether that net spread compensates for materially worse secondary liquidity. The exemption is the carry; the credit is the risk. Duration is medium-to-long (IG corporate CRAs commonly 4–10y+: Seara Oct-2035, Klabin May-2034, Minerva Nov-2034) — these are not short cash-like instruments; long IPCA+ duration creates real mark-to-market sensitivity to the NTN-B curve. Direct CRA is curve-marked off ANBIMA's indicative taxas de CRI e CRA, so measured volatility is a smoothing illusion — apply a liquidity haircut and treat measured vol as a lower bound.

05Key subtypes — the bimodal split is the thesis

The wrapper does not define the risk engine. The whole risk story is the split between IG-corporate / agroindustrial single-obligor paper — often just an exempt corporate financing — and the pulverized producer / trading / "creative-lastro" tail, which layers agronomic, weather, basis and regional-concentration risks that do not show up on a balance sheet.

Structure works — but it is an underwriting result, not a feature. The Aliça Agrícola do Cerrado case (Jan-2026) is the cleanest illustration on disk that structure, not the borrower's solvency, protects a CRA investor. Despite the devedor's operational collapse and entry into recuperação judicial, CRA holders were paid in full — R$114mn (principal + juros + multa + encargos) on 27-Jan-2026 — because guarantees were segregated (escrow account + CDA/WA soy warrants, outside the operating cash) and the vencimento-antecipado clause fired before the company could shield assets. This is the affirmative case for selective direct CRA — and the precise reason the opaque pulverized tail, with no real collateral visibility, is uninvestable for a direct buyer without a credit team.

06Key variables & risks

The crowd is leaning into the wrong sleeve at the wrong time. Retail (PF) ownership is at an all-time high — 25% of estoque, and 73.9% of CRA volume placed in May-2026 — precisely as the granular-producer credit cycle turns. Agro recuperações judiciais hit 1,990 in 2025 (+56% YoY, a record series), 853 from PF producers. The crowd is most exposed to the exact segment deteriorating fastest, chasing the headline IPCA+ as if "tax-exempt" were a free lunch. The regulatory tightening (CMN 5.118→5.212) is quietly improving new-issue quality, but the behavioral trap is live.

07Structuring: direct vs fund

This is the one genuinely split verdict among the exempt classes — conditional on subtype. Direct is selectively defensible only for the largest brAAA single-obligor IG deals held to maturity; the fund, with a credit team, is the only safe route for everything pulverized, trading, CPR-pooled or sub-IG.

When direct works
  • Only for the largest brAAA single-obligor IG names (Seara/JBS-aval, Marfrig, Klabin, Minerva, São Martinho), held to maturity.
  • The credit is transparent (continuous public disclosure of a listed obligor), the exemption is captured cleanly without a fee layer, single-name concentration can be sized acceptably, and holding to maturity neutralizes the illiquid secondary. Monitoring 1–3 IG names is feasible for a R$5mm PF.
  • Never direct for pulverized/producer paper — self-diversifying it would need dozens of names across obligors, regions and crops with continuous covenant monitoring, and adverse selection bites hardest at retail.
When a fund is the route
  • For pulverized, trading, CPR pools and any sub-IG paper, where the fund externalizes diversification (40–100 names), origination access (a manager anchoring >90% of a deal inverts adverse selection), active workout capacity (non-trivial in the RJ wave), and exchange liquidity.
  • The ~0.8–1.0% fee is small versus the IPCA+8–10% gross carry and the DD/illiquidity/adverse-selection it removes — it earns its fee on pulverized credit.
  • It is less justified for a single brAAA name a PF can hold directly, fee-free; and the FIDC / FIAGRO-FIDC route is taxed on income, failing the exempt thesis on tax grounds.
08Investment conviction & client fit

Verdict — Selective. CRAs belong in the portfolio, but not as one generic bucket and not as a CDI/cash substitute. The split is mandatory, and it is data-backed: by volume the market is large IG-corporate (R$272.8mn average deal); by stress it is the pulverized tail (1,990 agro RJ in 2025); structure pays through default only when escrow + CDA/WA + a diligent securitizadora are all real (Aliça recovered R$114mn at par); and retail is crowding the wrong sleeve at the wrong time (25% of estoque, 73.9% of monthly placement).

  • IG corporate single-obligor — Selective / Core. Exempt IPCA+ carry on a verifiable brAAA name held to maturity; the only Core-capable bucket, and the one where direct is defensible. Underwrite name-by-name (leverage, aval, duration, single-name concentration), not "agro" as a theme.
  • Pulverized and trading/input — Avoid direct. Adverse selection, unattainable diversification, correlated commodity/climate stress and a record RJ wave demanding workout capacity the PF lacks. Satellite only via a proven high-grade managed fund.
  • Subordinated tranches — Avoid. The PF buys the senior.
  • The CPR/collateral layer — a quality screen, not a bucket. A strong escrow + CDA-WA package is the difference between recovery and loss.

Fits if — the client wants exempt IPCA+ carry over a 10–20y horizon, can buy 1–3 large brAAA IG agro names directly and hold to maturity, and accesses pulverized/diversified agro credit only through a high-grade managed fund; treats CRA as a real-rate-plus-thin-credit sleeve, not appreciation.

Reduce or avoid if — treating "tax-exempt" as a free lunch; chasing the highest IPCA+ headline (the pulverized high-risk tail); needing liquidity (direct CRA exit is OTC/illiquid); unwilling to separate IG from producer risk; or expecting cash-like safety from a long-duration credit instrument. Increase if spreads widen materially above the credit or a clean brAAA gross-up over a taxable peer opens; decrease if spreads compress toward ~0.90%, the agro cycle broadens to corporates, or exemption reform advances.

Competes with. Versus NTN-B: CRA = NTN-B + a thin exempt spread, wins after gross-up for IG names net of illiquidity. Versus infra-debêntures / FI-Infra: those are generally more diversified, IG, and have both legs exempt — likely the larger exempt-IPCA+ core, with CRA the selective agro satellite. Versus CDI/cash: not a substitute. Versus offshore: BRL/local-credit, single-jurisdiction, single-sector — it concentrates Brazil-agro risk rather than diversifying it.
