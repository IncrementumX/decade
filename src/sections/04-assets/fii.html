01

Overview

Definition & tax
Definition
FIIs are Brazilian closed-end real-estate investment funds (broadly comparable to U.S. REITs), typically traded on the Brazilian stock exchange through quotas, that provide investors with indirect exposure to the real-estate market.
Underlying exposure

Varies materially across vehicles, as will be seen below:

  • Equity FIIs own income-generating real assets, such as malls, logistics warehouses and office buildings.
  • Credit / paper FIIs invest primarily in real-estate credit instruments, especially CRIs.
  • Hybrid portfolios combine both strategies.
  • FOFs (funds-of-funds) — FIIs can also be accessed through them.
Structuring
  • Closed-end vehicles — quotaholders do not have a standing redemption right against the fund.
  • Liquidity, when available, is typically obtained through exit in the secondary market.
Tax regimeTax efficiency is concentrated in income distributions, not in total return.
Income distributions

For Brazilian individuals, distributed income may be tax-exempt, provided:

  • the fund is traded on an exchange or organized OTC market;
  • it has at least 100 quotaholders (a threshold originally set at 50 and raised to 100 by Lei 14.754/2023, effective 1 January 2024);
  • the investor does not hold 10% or more of the quotas or economic rights to the fund's income.
Capital gains
  • Gains realized upon the sale of FII quotas are taxable, currently at a 20% rate on net gains.
  • As a result, FIIs are primarily tax-efficient income vehicles, while quota prices remain fully exposed to market risk.
Distribution policy
  • By law, FIIs must distribute at least 95% of profits calculated on a cash basis, based on semi-annual financial statements dated June 30 and December 31.
  • In practice, listed FIIs typically distribute income monthly, although the statutory distribution requirement is semi-annual — relevant for investors seeking recurring cash income and a more explicit pass-through of real-estate cash flows.
Benchmark
  • Equity FIIs — IFIX (B3's real-estate fund index, designed to track the average performance of listed real-estate funds).
  • Credit FIIs — managers adopt CDI or IPCA + spreads.

Value chain

How it is built

How a FII is originated, structured and monetized — from the underlying real asset or receivable through to the cash that reaches the quotaholder.

1

Underlying asset / receivable

The economic exposure sits in either real assets or real-estate credit instruments.

2

Origination / structuring

The opportunity is originated by an asset owner, developer or borrower, and structured by the manager and related service providers into an investable fund exposure.

3

FII vehicle

The FII is the closed-end listed vehicle that holds the assets and concentrates the underlying cash flows.

4

B3-listed quotas

Investors access the fund through exchange-traded quotas, typically buying and selling them in the secondary market.

5

Investor distributions

Underlying cash flows — rent, interest, monetary correction, amortization or realized gains — are collected by the fund, netted for expenses, and distributed to quotaholders.

04

Market structure

Size · liquidity

The FII market has moved from a niche income product to a mainstream listed asset class in Brazil. Over the past decade, the investor base expanded materially, the number of listed vehicles increased, and primary issuance reached institutional scale — making FIIs one of the clearest examples of the democratization of capital markets in the country.

Among the six tax-exempt asset classes analyzed in this case, FIIs are the most liquid and transparent implementation channel: quotas are exchange-traded, prices are observable daily, disclosure is standardized, and investors can access diversified real-estate exposure without committing capital to illiquid private assets or individual credit instruments.

FIIs have been trading at a discount to NAV because the market is repricing real-estate income under a higher cost of capital. The discount reflects not only elevated rates, but also skepticism around NAV quality, liquidity, dividend sustainability and asset-level risks. In this context, P/NAV below 1.0 is not automatically a valuation opportunity; it is a starting point for underwriting.

Key subtypes

Equity · Credit · Hybrid · FoF

While the FII universe includes several sub-strategies, this section focuses on the four categories that matter most for portfolio construction, given their different access routes and exposures across the underlying real-estate capital stack: Equity FIIs, Credit FIIs, Hybrid FIIs and FoFs.

Segment data · interactive

Every segment trades below NAV; the spread — from 0.68x in offices to 0.92x in urban income — is what drives relative conviction, not the discount alone. All segments compressed from end-2025 (see full data).

Where distribution yield sits above total return (credit: 15.3% DY vs 8.4%), the price leg detracted — de-rating or return-of-capital, not pure income. High yield alone is not wealth creation; read DY against total return.

Credit dominates by size and index weight but has thin per-fund liquidity — breadth, not depth. Urban income and hybrid punch above their weight; FoFs are effectively uninvestable at scale (R$0.6M ADTV per fund).

The index weight is the passive baseline. Conviction weight diverges deliberately: favour quality equity income, hold credit only at high grade, and underweight the cautious segments regardless of their index size.

Logistics

Selective · core

Listed equity in logistics and warehouse real estate. Income is contracted rent from single- and multi-tenant industrial leases (typical and atypical contracts), with structural demand support from e-commerce and supply-chain modernization. Underwrite it as real-estate equity, not fixed income.

Key variables to monitor
Location qualityProximity to São Paulo, last-mile reach and major corridors drive rent and occupancy.
Tenant quality & concentrationA few large tenants raise renewal and default risk.
WALEWeighted average lease term; longer terms mean more contracted, visible income.
VacancyPhysical and financial vacancy against the rent roll.
Rent growth vs inflationWhether contracted indexation is actually being passed through.
P/VP & implied cap rateEntry valuation and the yield the assets throw off at today's price.
Manager & feesOrigination discipline and net-of-fee yield to the investor.

Malls

Selective · core

Listed equity in shopping centres. Income from base rent, percentage rent on tenant sales, plus parking and ancillary revenue. Reported yield runs below other equity segments because mall managers reinvest operating cash into acquisitions and new projects — a lower headline yield in exchange for embedded growth.

Key variables to monitor
Mall tier & ABL qualityDominant Tier-1 assets defend rents through the cycle; weak malls do not.
Tenant sales & NOI growthThe operating engine behind rent and percentage rent.
Occupancy / vacancyFootfall and tenant health.
Percentage rentUpside linked directly to retailer sales.
Cap rate / P/VPEntry valuation versus replacement and NAV.
AcquisitionsWhether M&A is accretive or dilutive to value per quota.

Urban income

Selective · core

Listed equity in income-producing urban operating assets leased to single tenants on long-term, usually inflation-linked contracts — supermarkets, retail strips, education and agency/retail-logistics properties — typically via sale-leaseback or built-to-suit (atypical) structures. Long lease terms and contracted, inflation-pass-through rent make this among the cleanest exempt real-estate-income engines.

Key variables to monitor
Tenant credit & concentrationSingle-tenant net leases concentrate counterparty risk in one name.
Contract typeTypical vs atypical (built-to-suit / sale-leaseback) changes tenant lock-in and break risk.
IndexationIPCA or IGP-M pass-through on the contracted rent.
WALELong leases anchor the visibility of income.
Re-letting riskCost and vacancy exposure if a single tenant exits.
P/VP & DY coverageLeast-discounted equity segment; test whether the yield is actually covered.

Offices

Cautious · avoid as core

Listed equity in corporate office buildings. The most-discounted IFIX segment (P/VP ~0.68x), reflecting structural demand impairment — hybrid work, oversupply in secondary submarkets — and skepticism on NAV quality. The thesis here is discount-closing and occupancy recovery, i.e. price appreciation — and appreciation on a quota is taxed at 20%. The wrapper helps least exactly where the screen looks cheapest: most of the upside is taxed gain, not exempt income.

Key variables to monitor
SubmarketPrime (Faria Lima, Itaim, JK) holds value; secondary São Paulo and Rio do not.
Physical vacancyThe value-trap signature — high, persistent vacancy.
Asking vs effective rentConcessions hide genuinely weak demand.
Lease maturitiesNear-term expiries into a soft market raise re-letting risk.
P/VP discountHow much pessimism is already priced.
DY coverageMarket DY can sit well above covered (patrimonial) DY.
Liquidation vs NAVWhat the buildings are really worth versus carrying value.

Credit / paper

Investable · high grade only

Diversified pools of real-estate credit — primarily CRIs — that pay an exempt monthly coupon. Economically these are outsourced credit funds with exchange liquidity, not real-estate equity: benchmark them as credit (against CDI, NTN-B and a direct-CRI portfolio), not against a building's rent. Return = indexer (CDI or IPCA) + spread − defaults − fees ± duration / mark-to-market.

High grade

Senior-secured receivables, strong sponsors, low loan-to-value, tight spreads. Lowest default risk — the core of the sleeve.

Medium grade

Moderate spreads, mixed seniority and sponsor quality. Useful carry, but it warrants closer monitoring.

Low grade / high yield

Wide spreads for weaker structures — real default and adverse-selection risk; the manager must not be warehousing the paper everyone else passed on.

Key variables to monitor
Indexer & durationCDI (short, re-rates down as Selic falls) vs IPCA (longer, marks to the NTN-B curve).
Credit spreadCompensation above the indexer for the risk taken.
Loan-to-valueThe collateral cushion behind each receivable.
CollateralFiduciary lien / assignment and reserve funds backing the structures.
Default / restructuring historyRealised credit quality, not just the headline carry.
ConcentrationBorrower and sector diversification across the pool.
Reinvestment riskRedeploying amortisations at lower spreads in a cutting cycle.

Hybrid

Not core

Hybrid FIIs hold both legs — equity and credit — in one ticker, with the manager shifting the mix. Convenient as a single-ticket allocation, but the moving mandate makes the underlying exposure harder to underwrite and to coordinate with the rest of the sleeve. Conviction: not core — it is better to hold specialised equity and credit funds directly, choosing the industry and the layer of the capital stack, than to outsource that mix to a hybrid mandate.

Key variables to monitor
Equity-vs-credit splitThe current mix and how far the mandate drifts over time.
Underlying qualityAsset and credit quality of each leg.
FeesWhether the convenience is paid for in a higher fee load.
OverlapDouble-counting against the rest of the FII sleeve.
Allocation track recordWhether the manager has actually added value by shifting the mix.

Funds-of-funds

Not core

FoFs hold a basket of other FIIs, offering single-ticket diversification, manager allocation across the universe and discount-to-NAV arbitrage. Conviction: not core — the fee structure is redundant: a fund-level fee sits on top of the fees already charged by the underlying funds (double layering), reducing net distributable yield. An advisor building the portfolio against a fund checklist can do that selection directly, without the extra layer.

Key variables to monitor
Double-layer feesFund-level fee on top of the underlying funds' fees.
Underlying overlapHow much the basket duplicates positions held directly.
Selection skillWhether the manager's picks beat a simple direct mix net of fees.
Discount-to-NAV entryThe only real edge — buying the basket below the sum of its parts.
LiquidityThe thinnest segment of the market.
06

Risks

Variables · conflicts

The variables are grouped by what they decide. Macro factors set whole-class entry and exit; the segment-specific factors decide which subtypes and which managers to hold. Severity is the typical weight on the decision — H high · M medium · L low.

Macro · drives whole-class entry & exit
Rates · Selic · NTN-BH
FIIs re-rate with the rate cycle. A clear cutting cycle is the principal catalyst; a hawkish turn the principal drawdown.
P/VP discountM
A re-rating option and a standing behavioural risk that can deepen before it closes.
Tax & regulatoryM
The income exemption is statutory and has been debated in reform cycles; capital gains are already taxed at 20%.
Liquidity concentrationM
A handful of names carry most volume; the long tail is thin and hard to build or exit at size.
Equity — specific
Vacancy · WALE · tenant concentrationMHigh, persistent vacancy is the value-trap signature (offices); single large tenants raise renewal risk.
Sector & demandMOversupply in weaker logistics regions, the consumer cycle in malls, structural WFH and oversupply in offices.
Asset quality & cap rateMOverpaying for "quality"; cap-rate expansion if real rates stay high.
Credit — specific
Credit & defaultMA receivable default or restructuring hits net assets and distributions; worse in high-yield names.
Duration · mark-to-marketHIPCA names (4–14y) swing with the NTN-B curve — the dominant short-term risk for paper.
ReinvestmentMCDI carry compresses as Selic falls; amortisations redeploy at lower spreads.
Adverse selectionMUnderwriting is outsourced — the manager must not be warehousing the paper everyone else passed on.
Cross-cutting · all subtypes
Manager & governanceM
Discretion over leverage, related-party deals and dilutive follow-on emissions.
Fee dragM
Management plus performance fees erode the net exempt yield to the investor.
BehaviouralM
A largely retail base prices off the monthly coupon, amplifying price-leg volatility and sustaining discounts.
Conflicts of interest — the fund-selection lens
AUM growth incentive
Managers are typically paid as a percentage of fund assets, creating an incentive to raise capital and acquire assets even when incremental deals are not clearly accretive to existing quotaholders.
Related-party transactions
Funds may acquire properties, CRIs or services from affiliated entities. Investors should assess the related-party policy, approval process, pricing discipline and percentage of NAV exposed to affiliated transactions.
Follow-on dilution
New issuances can dilute existing investors if priced below fair value or used to fund acquisitions at unattractive cap rates / spreads. Track issuance price vs NAV, use of proceeds and expected accretion.
Performance-fee incentives
Hurdles such as CDI or IPCA+ spreads may encourage yield-chasing, especially in credit FIIs, if the manager is rewarded for short-term carry rather than long-term credit quality.
Asset recycling decisions
In equity FIIs, buy/sell decisions can benefit the manager's advisory platform or AUM growth agenda. The key test is whether disposals and acquisitions improve portfolio quality and long-term cash-flow generation per quota.
Fee drag and double layering
Management fees, performance fees, structuring fees and, in FoFs, fees on underlying funds reduce distributable yield. The relevant metric is net yield to the investor, not gross asset yield.
07

Structuring

Direct vs fund

For FIIs the "direct vs fund" question barely arises: the listed FII is already the fund. There is no fund layer to add or strip out — the only real decision is which listed vehicle to hold.

The route
Listed FII
The fund is the ticker: daily exchange liquidity, exempt monthly income and diversified, professionally managed real-estate exposure in a single quota.
Not for this investor
Direct property
Buying a building directly forfeits the two things that make the exposure work — daily liquidity and the income-tax exemption (direct rental income is taxed) — while adding hands-on asset and tenant management.
So the decision is fund selection — what to check
  • Fees — management and performance fees, net of which the exempt yield must still stand up.
  • Issuance history — follow-on emissions priced below NAV dilute existing holders.
  • Liquidity — enough daily volume to build and exit the position cleanly.
  • Manager & governance — track record, related-party discipline and alignment with quotaholders.

Conviction

Verdict · persona
Verdict
FIIs earn a place — selectively and by subtype, not as one generic bucket. Equity FIIs and credit FIIs are investable, conditional on manager quality, asset and credit quality, and entry valuation; offices, hybrids and FoFs are not core.
Equity — logistics, malls, urban incomeInvestableExempt real-asset income; underwrite asset quality and entry valuation name by name.
Equity — officesAvoid as coreA turnaround whose upside is taxed capital gain, not exempt income — the wrong tax fit.
Credit / paperInvestable · HG onlyDiversified, liquid, exempt access to real-estate credit — at high grade only.
HybridNot coreBetter to hold specialised equity and credit funds directly than outsource the mix.
Funds-of-fundsNot coreRedundant, double-layered fees; the selection can be done directly.
When it works
  • A clear Selic-cutting cycle re-rates quotas.
  • Income-payers at fair P/VP with covered distributions compound tax-free.
  • Widening discounts with stable fundamentals create asymmetric entry.
When it fails
  • A hawkish rate turn hits duration (equity and IPCA paper).
  • The thesis leans on the taxed capital-gain leg (deep-discount offices).
  • Discounts compress without fundamental support; fee and emission drag erode the carry.
Who it serves
  • An investor wanting exempt monthly income with daily liquidity and real-asset diversification.
  • One who accepts genuine drawdown in exchange for honest marks.
  • Whose thesis is recurring income, not NAV re-rating.
Who should avoid
  • Anyone treating FIIs as a CDI or fixed-income substitute (a category error).
  • Those relying on the taxed appreciation leg.
  • Those needing capital-preservation certainty, or able to trade only the thin tail.
